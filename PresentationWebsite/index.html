GoCart

Faster and Cheaper


GoCart is a grocery delivery app that allows shoppers to source the cheapest possible grocery order from all available stores in the vicinity, as well as being used to place an order at any nearby grocery stores.

Designed for

Customers who are looking for convenience. That is the convenience of not actually having to physically go to the store, and not needing to look for the best prices.


This can include

Working parents who wish to save time so that they can spend more time with their family

Elderly people who want their groceries delivered due to difficulties traveling to the grocery store

College students who wish to save money on their groceries

Features

Find stores your way


GoCart allows you to find a store either by name, or by proximity to your address


Find products easily


Finding products in grocery stores can be done by name. Products can also be categorized with categories, and as well as limiting which products are allergen free.


Optimize your order instanlty


On a shopping cart, users have the option to find the cheapest stores for their order. GoCart will look for the stores with the best prices for the user's items, and present the user with an optimized shopping cart filled with the best possible prices for their order.


Save your information


GoCart allows users to create their own account so that they can store and access their information.

What did you learn about team or large project development? What will you start doing, keep doing, or stop doing next time?

As a team working on GoCart, we learned about how important communication is to the success of developing a large project. Working on a team project minimally impacted our growth in programming, however we learned a lot of lessons about working with a group. Hence what we will keep doing, stop doing, and start doing is rooted in communication.


Our success in each iteration correlated with how much we communicated throughout the month. In the first iteration, we held meetings frequently. These meetings, although short, allowed us to gauge each other’s progress. Team members who knew they were behind picked up their phase, and members who were ahead were known. Members who needed help knew who had extra time to help them out, and overall, there was less anxiety about the upcoming due date since everyone knew each other’s progress. Hence holding scheduled meetings weekly is something we would keep doing if there are future iterations.


Contrast that to the second iteration. In the second iteration, we only started contacting each other around one week prior to the iteration’s release date. This was mostly due to communication issues since either one or two people would back out right before the designated meeting time, which would lead to the cancellation of a meeting. For a future iteration, we need to stop cancelling these meetings over just one person missing. We cannot control the unexpected situations that arise in each other’s lives, and as a group we should not collapse over just one person missing. This issue could have been solved easily by just sending the missing member a message about what was discussed.


Throughout the development of each iteration, we noticed that the work of team members trended towards one part of the application. Members preferred to work on either database, user interface, or testing, but not more than one of those. We learned that people are unique, and different people specialize at different things. We believe our individual talents are not being used efficiently by assigning each other features in isolation. We should be working on features together, focusing on specialization. The biggest reason that we could not do this was due to never having a conversation about it. No one was comfortable enough to introduce such a drastic change, and we did not yet have the trust to entrust each other parts of our work. But now after three iterations we know what each other is good at, and if there were a future iteration, we would focus on specialization over working individually on our own features. The takeaway here is that in a large project development, different people will specialize in different things. As a group, it is important to figure out early which role best suits a member rather than relying each member to be a jack of all trades.

How did the project change from your initial (iteration 0) vision or stories, or did it work out as predicted?

For the most part, we were able to deliver most of the features we initially proposed in iteration 0. We encountered some hiccups in iteration 2. Part of it was due to the last day crunch, and the other was due to the features in this iteration being too big. This led to user stories being removed from the feature “Create user accounts”. The user stories we cut did not have a huge effect on our vision since they were mostly quality of life changes and can easily be reintroduced in a later iteration on a separate feature.

We also concluded that our vision for the project has been satisfied for iteration 3. The main and unique functionality that we promised, which is finding the cheapest order, has been fully implemented. After the completion of that feature, we were confident and fully committed to the vision.
